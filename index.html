Step 1 — Research & Discovery

We began by exploring how to make health education more effective.
Research showed that social environments play a vital role —
children are more motivated to exercise when doing it together.
We also learned that childhood obesity is
an increasingly serious concern, inspiring us
to focus our design on young learners.

Step 2 — Choosing the Right Medium

Games capture children’s attention better
than traditional lessons. This insight led us to adopt a
game-based learning approach — where fun meets education!

Step 3 — Engaging with Teachers

To ensure classroom suitability, we surveyed preschool teachers. Their advice:
• Keep visuals colourful and simple
• Encourage group interaction
• Use movement-based play to support learning

Step 4 — Early Prototype

Our first prototype introduced a simple, exciting mechanic:
Healthy and unhealthy foods fall from the screen, and children
move in front of the camera to “catch” the healthy foods.
✅ Gain points for good choices
❌ Lose points for unhealthy ones
It was fun, active, and educational — but we wanted more collaboration.

Step 5 — Iteration & Feedback

Feedback revealed that players wanted more teamwork.
So we evolved our game into a collaborative classroom experience:
Children must work together to move into position and catch healthy foods as a group.
If too many players are out of sync — the catch fails!
This promotes cooperation, coordination, and shared success.
